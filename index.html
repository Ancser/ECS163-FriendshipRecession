Key Pattern

The pre-COVID collapse. Between 2014–2017 socializing dropped 10.9% in 3 years before any pandemic — aligning exactly with mass adoption of Snapchat and Instagram.

Core Insight

Structural, not personal. The decline was system-wide six years before COVID. Something in the fabric of American life had already begun to erode.

Youth & Men

Ages 15–24 lost 54% of their social time. Many entered adulthood after the architecture for making friends had already collapsed. Men's zero-friend rate quintupled from 3% to 15%.

Race & Income

Higher-income, college-educated groups show steeper percentage declines — work demands scale with income. Black and Hispanic Americans show smaller declines, potentially reflecting stronger community structures.

The Screen Flood

Alone-with-screens absorbed the largest share of lost social time. Social media promised connection while delivering passive consumption. The amber socializing stream narrows exactly as the leisure/screen stream expands.

Why Social Time?

Coordination cost makes social time fragile. Sleep is non-negotiable. Work has deadlines. But meeting a friend requires mutual scheduling and effort — and that friction compounds over time.

What Could Be

John, restored.

If the UK's social prescribing model were adopted nationally, Americans would gain back an estimated 3.7 hours of social time per week.

If co-housing expanded to 2% of housing stock, the zero-friends rate could fall from 17% toward single digits.

If public third places were invested in at Finland's per-capita rate, millions of Johns would have somewhere to simply be with other people.
